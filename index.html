What It Is

TubStep™ is a drain insert that fits into any standard 1.5" bathtub drain. When a user steps on the device, an internal rotary blade mechanism activates and cuts hair into small pieces — no touching required.

The device is made from five stainless steel 316 parts, fully precision machined and assembled by hand. Installation requires no tools: drop it in, and it's ready.

Why We Made It

Hair clogs are the #1 cause of residential drain blockages, affecting an estimated 40 million Americans. Existing solutions — like TubShroom and Press Drain — either require users to handle accumulated hair, or fail to capture it at all.

TubStep doesn't collect hair. It shreds it. The pieces are small enough to flush through the plumbing system without accumulating.

How It Works

Foot pressure drives a cam mechanism that converts linear motion to shaft rotation. The rotating shaft drives overlapping blades in a scissor-shear action, cutting hair at the point of entry. When you lift your foot, a return spring resets the mechanism. Water flushes the fragments down. The whole cycle takes under a second.

[ 01 / CAM MECHANISM ]

The Cam

The cam is the heart of the motion-conversion system. When the user applies downward foot pressure to the top face of the device, the cam translates that linear force into rotation at the central shaft. The cam profile was designed to produce a full shearing rotation within the travel range of a typical foot press — approximately 10–15 mm of linear displacement.

Rotary blade assembly
Replace: assets/proto-blades.jpg

[ 02 / ROTARY BLADES ]

The Blades

The rotary blade assembly rotates with the keyed shaft. Shearing occurs at the contact line where the rotating blades overlap with the fixed blade geometry — a scissor-style shear that severs hair cleanly at the point of entry. The blade geometry was validated through a razor blade proof-of-concept test before the final stainless steel parts were machined.

Return spring and adjustment nut
Replace: assets/proto-spring.jpg

[ 03 / SPRING + NUT ]

The Spring & Nut

A compression spring on the shaft provides the return force that resets the cam and blades after each actuation. The adjustment nut allows the preload — and therefore the return force — to be tuned at assembly. This also prevents hair from wrapping and jamming the shaft over time, a failure mode observed in earlier prototypes.

Outer bowl / housing
Replace: assets/proto-bowl.jpg

[ 04 / OUTER BOWL ]

The Bowl

The outer bowl serves as the structural housing and water-management component. Its geometry channels water flow through the blade region while collecting any larger hair fragments before they reach the trap. The 1.5" outer diameter matches standard residential bathtub drains, requiring no modification for installation.

Fully assembled prototype

[ 05 / FULL ASSEMBLY ]

Full Assembly

Five stainless steel 316 parts. Fully machined. Assembled by hand in minutes. The assembled device sits flush in a standard drain and is ready to use immediately — no adhesives, no fasteners beyond the adjustment nut, no modifications to the plumbing. The entire mechanism is accessible for inspection or cleaning by simply lifting it out.

The problem is real.
The solutions aren't.

Cause of residential drain blockages

Hair accumulation in residential drains is the leading cause of blockages in bathtubs and showers. Roughly 40 million Americans report dealing with frequent drain clogs, creating a persistent maintenance burden and a substantial market for drain solutions.

The existing market is dominated by passive collection devices — products that trap hair and require users to periodically remove and dispose of accumulated waste. These solutions shift the problem rather than solving it, and nearly all require direct contact with wet, tangled hair.

TubStep™ takes a different approach: active elimination rather than passive collection. By shredding hair at the point of entry, the problem never accumulates.

Simulations & Experiments

Engineering the
details.

Three quantitative studies informed key design decisions in TubStep™ — from blade geometry to dimensional compliance with federal safety standards.

[ STUDY 01 / FLUID MECHANICS ] Minimum Drain Area for Water Flow
Question

How much open drain area is required to prevent water backup during normal shower use?

Method

Shower flow capped at 0.158 kg/s per code. Assumed 1 inch of hydrostatic head pressure. Applied pressure-driven flow equations to calculate minimum open area through the drain geometry with a factor of safety of 1.5.

Result
0.869 in²
minimum open area  ·  equivalent diameter: 1.052"
Design Decision

TubStep™ blade geometry was designed to maintain more than 0.869 in² of open area in every possible blade position — including worst-case rotation angles.

[ STUDY 02 / MECHANICS OF MATERIALS ] Blade Shear Force & Scissor Mechanics
Question

What blade geometry produces effective and reliable hair shearing under realistic foot-pressure loads?

Method

Blade interaction modeled as scissor-style shearing — shear force concentrated at the contact line between overlapping blades. A razor blade proof-of-concept prototype was built and tested to validate the cutting force and geometry before final parts were machined.

Result
Rotary V2
design confirmed — clean shear at blade overlap under foot-load
Design Decision

The rotary V2 overlapping-blade design was selected over a linear reciprocating blade approach. The rotary method produces a more consistent shear contact line and requires lower peak force to initiate cutting.

[ STUDY 03 / STANDARDS COMPLIANCE ] Safety & Dimensional Standards
Question

Does TubStep™ meet applicable federal, ASME, and ASTM standards for dimensional and safety compliance?

Method

Checked TubStep™ dimensions against four applicable standards:

  • ✓
    ASME A112.18.2 §4.6.1.2
    Min. 1.5" nominal OD for bathtub drains — TubStep: 1.5"
  • ✓
    ASTM A554-21 Table 2
    Wall thickness 0.025"–0.134" for 1.5" OD steel — TubStep: 0.095"
  • ✓
    CFR 16 §1500.49
    Blade gaps must be < 0.34" — TubStep: 0.337"
  • ✓
    ASTM F409-22
    Material & wall thickness for tubular plumbing components — Pass
Result
4 / 4
all standards checks pass  ·  blade gaps ≤ 0.337"
Design Decision

Razor blades were eliminated from the final design in favor of the machined rotary mechanism, which naturally constrains all blade-access gaps to ≤ 0.337" — meeting the CPSC safety rule with 0.003" margin.
